Project Overview

This project involved undertaking user research and partially redesigning a website for an Australian not-for-profit organisation that would be responsive across two different devices. Assistive Technology Australia was selected as the not-for-profit organisation for this task, and the website was redesigned to be responsive across iPhone 8 and desktop screens.

The approach taken for this project focused on:

  1. Simplifying the process of registering for a course
  2. Streamlining the payment process by adopting an online payment system instead of completing a PDF form and sending it back to the organisation.
  3. Replacing the browsealoud accessibility toolbar with the the UserWay floating accessibility menu
  4. Developing and implementing a style guide/design system

Please note that this project was completed as part of a design course assessment with set guidelines and required deliverables. It is not affiliated with Assistive Technology Australia and no members of the design team were paid or otherwise recompensed for their work.

Background

"Assistive technology" is a term which is used to describe tools & technologies used by the elderly or people experiencing disability to help them perform activities. A wide range of products can fall into this category including non-slip mats, walking sticks, wheelchairs, an app that can helps people with vision-impairment to perform daily tasks, and more.

The original homepage of the Assistive Technology Australia website on desktop

"Our purpose is to provide impartial advice, information and leadership that builds capacity and optimises the value of assistive technology in leading a life of choice."
- Assistive Technology Australia


Assistive Technology Australia:

  1. Provides informative documents about assistive technology
  2. Has a product database of assistive technology products in the Australian market (although these not purchaseable via the Assistive Technology Australia website)
  3. Offers several courses about how to provide support to people experiencing disability

Research

Data was collected from four interviews and one semi-structured survey that had 9 respondents. The interview and survey questions were focused on their understanding and usage of assistive technology and website accessibilty features. Most of the interviewees had an elederly relative that used/uses assistive technology. Also, one interviewee suffers from lupus and another was a special needs assistant at a school. The special needs assistant was familiar some assistive technology products that were used to help students in their learning. The interview data were collated into an affinity diagram. From this, we were able to extract findings, needs/wants and pain points of the users.

Affinity map containing the data from the 5 interviews, coloured by insights, wants/needs and pain points mentioned by the inverviewees

Ideation & Brainstorming

Defining the user and the problem:

For this project, the user persona (that is, an imagined user of the re-designed website) developed was a school teacher who wants to find about tools that can assist some of her students with learning difficulties who are falling behind in their studies. She also wants to do some professional training in this area which suits her work schedule.

The user persona, a primary school teacher, Catherine Phillips, who wants to better support challenged students who are struggling to keep up in class

UX hypothesis:

"Through the Assistive Technology Australia website, people seeking information about assistive technology can easily find what they need and discover tools and resources to support themselves and others with accessibility needs."

UX insight:

"Catherine is a primary school special needs educator and is committed to staying up to date on the latest assistive technology to ensure her students have access to the best tools to further their education."

The user scenario shows the hypothetical steps that the user persona, Catherine, may go through which leads to her using the Assistive Technology Australia website.

Problem statment:

"Potential users, educators & caregivers are not aware of what assistive technology resources are available. How might we redesign the Assistive Technology Australia website to ensure users are aware of the comprehensive information provided, education and assistance available?"

Value proposition:

"To provide an online accessible platform for people to find resources about assistive technology, allowing them to implement information and tools that improve their quality of life."

Testing & Improving

In the research phase of the project, a remote guerilla usability test of the current Assistive Technology Australia desktop website was conducted. Three participants voluntary took part in this test over Zoom in order to indentify some of the areas for improvement. Some notable findings from this test found:

  1. There was no filtering system to narrow down the number of training courses
  2. The process of registering for a training course was somewhat inconvenient. Users must complete a general registration PDF form and then email it back to Assistive Technology Australia. The user must also provide their payment information (credit card details) in this form
  3. The accessibility toolbar was difficult to locate and operate. It was somewhat discrete and lacked descriptive features or onboarding to help the user understand how it functions

The mid-fidelity clickable mobile prototype was also tested in a remote guerilla test over Zoom before the high-fidelity mobile and desktop prototypes were developed.
The main feedback from this test was:

  1. Increasing the font size
  2. Adding a prompt to the accessibility widget
  3. Provide some more spacing between elements so that it does not look congested

A/B testing was conducted to see users' preference of certain aspects of the high-fidelity mobile prototype. Three user took part in this remote guerilla test over Zoom. These changes were then implemented in the high-fidelity mobile and desktop prototypes.

Future Direction

  1. Conduct usability test of the redesigned website with people who have disabilities
  2. Extend website redesign (such as to include navigating the product database
  3. Adapt the prototype to more screen sizes ( versions above iPhone 8 and android devices)
  4. Consider implementing keyboard shortcuts for the accessibility features & other website functions

Summary & Outcomes

  1. Website redesign that is responsive on two screen sizes (iPhone 6/7/8 and desktop)
  2. More consistent styling and layout across pages
  3. Adding a filter to organise training courses
  4. Registering for a training course with an online form instead of completing pdf form that is (scanned and) emailed back to Assistive Technology Australia
  5. Payment can be made through website with multiple payment options (Mastercard, Visa, PayPal or AfterPay)
  6. Implementing a different accessibility widget (UserWay) that is easier to understand & use
